More information about Climate Equity Scorecards

Each Equity Scorecard offers a quick look at a country’s international mitigation pledge, its formally professed willingness to act to stabilize the global climate. This pledge is expressed as a percentage, which is calculated by comparing the country’s pledge to its fair share of the international effort needed to close the gap between the current global emissions pathway and one of the global mitigation pathways. The country’s fair share is calculated within the Greenhouse Development Rights (GDRs) effort-sharing framework, and is based on the responsibility and capacity of all countries to contribute to the general effort of stabilizing the climate.

Level of Global Ambition – Global Mitigation Pathways

The Equity Scorecards system evaluates national pledges relative to the global effort that will be needed to achieve a target global mitigation pathway. Various target global emissions pathways can be used. They vary by level of ambitiousness, extent of risk, and whether effort is front-loaded or back-loaded, for example. We have chosen to highlight three mitigation pathways in these scorecards. (Many others, including one that’s more ambitious than the AOSIS pathway, are available in the GDRs calculator, which underlies the Scorecards system.

AOSIS
This is the most ambitious global mitigation pathway with high-level political support. It is drawn from the negotiating position of the Alliance of Small Island States (AOSIS), which requires “global greenhouse gas emissions to peak by 2015 and decline thereafter” and “reductions in global greenhouse gas emissions by more than 85% below 1990 levels by 2050.”

This, our moderate-ambition reference pathway, is just barely stringent enough to meet the IPCC guidance for an event or phenomenon that is likely to occur. Which is to say that, by current reckoning, it has a 66% chance of holding mean planetary warming below 2째C.

This global mitigation pathway has a cumulative budget that suggests a roughly 50% chance of temperatures rising above 2째C. This assessment is made in accordance with the analysis of Meinshausen et al (2009).

Type of Pledge

Many countries have expressed their national emissions mitigation pledges in the form of an unconditional pledge and a conditional pledge. The unconditional pledge is of lower ambition, but will be met unilaterally. The conditional pledge is higher ambition, but is conditional on other countries satisfying certain requirements relating to increasing their own ambition with respect to mitigation pledges and/or the provision of finance and technology.

Fair Share

The Equity Scorecards system assesses a country’s pledges by comparing them to a Greenhouse Development Rights based estimate of its fair share of the global climate effort. GDRs is an effort-sharing framework that aims to provide a plausible, rigorous and transparent quantification of the commitment by United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change signatories to “protect the climate system for the benefit of present and future generations of humankind, on the basis of equity and in accordance with their common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities.” (Note, from any Scorecard, the “more results for this country” link takes you to the GDRs calculator, where you can examine and experiment with a much more detailed analysis).

Pledged Effort

Nations can act to protect the climate system in one of two ways. They can act within their own borders, and they can act by providing international support to other countries. There are of course numerous complexities involved in qualifying and quantifying national actions, but at the end of the day they will have this dual aspect.

  • We do not attempt to estimate levels of domestic effort, but rather take authoritative estimates of pledged effort “off the shelf” as they become available. This raises an important caveat – the scores here are only meaningful to the extent that national pledges are made in good faith. On this matter, we attempt no judgments. Also note that in some cases a country is judged to be pledging NO effort. This is typically because it has pledged only to an emission intensity target that is smaller than its projected business-as-usual rate of intensity improvement.
  • International effort will also be essential in any successful climate regime, but to our knowledge, no nations have yet made international pledges that are precise enough to score. This is because the scores here reflect pledged effort in 2020, not (cumulative) pledged effort before 2020. The situation on this front will evolve rapidly in the years ahead. If and when it clarifies to the necessary point, international efforts will be added to these scores.

Pledge Gap

The gap shown in these Equity Scorecards represents the difference between a country’s pledged mitigation and its fair share of the global effort.

Business as Usual

Business-as-Usual baselines (“Business-as-Usual” pathways) are based on projected emissions from McKinsey and Company (Version 2.1). We update and calibrate McKinsey’s projections to be consistent with the most current available annual emissions data, based on fossil fuel CO2 from CDIAC’s data, non-CO2 GHGs from UNFCCC and USEPA, and land-use CO2 from Climate Analysis Indicators Tool.

See more detailed BAU documentation.

Emissions Intensity

Emissions Intensity is a measure of the efficiency of a country’s economic activity, in terms of greenhouse gas emitted per unit of economic output. It is defined as greenhouse-gas emissions divided by GDP, and can be calculated either in CO2-only terms or in terms of “all greenhouse gases.”

Responsibility

Greenhouse Development Rights defines responsibility – contribution to the climate problem – as the sum of all emissions corresponding to consumption above the development threshold. Emissions corresponding to consumption below that threshold are associated with basic consumption and therefore exempt from any contribution to national climate obligations. Responsibility is accumulated, in the GDRs standard case, from a 1990 start date. To the extent that emissions before the start date contributed to current capacity, they are accounted for implicitly in the capacity measure. To calculate national obligation, GDRs combines responsibility and capacity to compute a Responsibility and Capacity Index.

Capacity

Greenhouse Development Rights defines capacity as income not demanded by the necessities of life, so it is considered available to support the common global mitigation and adaptation effort. Aggregate capacity is calculated as the sum of all individual incomes, excluding income below the development threshold. This capacity is combined with responsibility to calculate a Responsibility and Capacity Index, which is taken as a measure of obligation to act. Note that capacity, as a measure of national wealth, is an indirect measure for historical emissions.

Responsibility and Capacity Index (RCI)

Greenhouse Development Rights combines measures of responsibility and capacity (using, in the standard case, a 50/50 weighting) into a composite indicator of national obligation called the Responsibility and Capacity Index, or RCI. RCI is used to compute the fair shares division of global mitigation costs in the GDRs framework. Because both responsibility and capacity are calculated in terms of the development threshold, a country’s RCI is indirectly affected by its internal income distribution, as well as by wealth and historical emissions. GDRs, interpreted as a tax, is moderately progressive. A dollar of income that is just above the development threshold is taxed at the same rate as a dollar earned by a billionaire.

Development Threshold

The development threshold is in many ways the key to the Greenhouse Development Rights framework. It defines an income threshold below which an individual, whatever country they may reside in, is taken to be exempt from international climate-related obligations. This threshold is emphatically not an “extreme poverty” line, which is typically defined to be so low ($1 or $2 a day) as to be more properly called a “destitution line.” Rather, it is set to be much higher ($20 per person per day, or $7,500 per person per year), which is a bit above a reasonably-defined “global poverty line.” That is to say, it reflects a level of welfare that is beyond basic needs but well short of today’s levels of “affluent” consumption.
